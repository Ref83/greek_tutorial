# Personal and Possessive Pronouns (Weak Forms)

## The Concept
In Greek, we often use shortened, unstressed versions of pronouns called **Weak Forms** (clitics). These are used to avoid repetition and to make sentences flow more naturally. They represent the "me, you, him/her/it" (Personal) or "my, your, his/her/its" (Possessive).

## Usage
* **Personal Pronouns (Weak):** Used as direct or indirect objects of a verb (e.g., "He sees **me**").
* **Possessive Pronouns (Weak):** Used to show ownership (e.g., "My **house**").

## The Mechanics

### 1. Personal Pronouns (Object Forms)
These forms depend on whether the pronoun is the **Direct Object** (Accusative) or **Indirect Object** (Genitive/Dative function).

| Person | Accusative (Direct) | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1st Sing.** | με | me |
| **2nd Sing.** | σε | you |
| **3rd Sing.** | τον / την / το | him / her / it |
| **1st Plur.** | μας | us |
| **2nd Plur.** | σας | you (all) |
| **3rd Plur.** | τους / τις (τες) / τα | them |

**Placement Rules:**
* **Before the Verb:** In simple statements.
    * *Example:* **Με** βλέπεις. (You see **me**.)
    * *Example:* **Τον** ξέρω. (I know **him**.)
* **After the Verb:** Only with **Imperatives** (commands) or **Gerunds**.
    * *Example:* Δώσε **μου**! (Give **me**!)
    * *Example:* Βλέποντάς **τον**... (Seeing **him**...)

### 2. Possessive Pronouns
The weak possessive pronouns are identical to the Genitive forms of the personal pronouns.

| Person | Form | Translation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1st Sing.** | μου | my |
| **2nd Sing.** | σου | your |
| **3rd Sing.** | του / της / του | his / her / its |
| **1st Plur.** | μας | our |
| **2nd Plur.** | σας | your (plural/formal) |
| **3rd Plur.** | τους | their |

**Placement Rules:**
* **After the Noun:** They always follow the noun they belong to.
    * *Example:* Το σπίτι **μου**. (My house.)
    * *Example:* Η φίλη **σου**. (Your friend.)

---

## Exceptions & Special Notes
* **The "ν" rule:** The masculine pronoun **τον** and the feminine **την** always keep the final "ν". However, in older rules, "την" would drop the "ν" if the next word started with certain consonants. In modern usage, keeping it is generally safer for clarity.
* **Double Accents:** If a word is accented on the third syllable from the end (antepenultimate), and is followed by a weak possessive pronoun, it gains a second accent.
    * *Example:* Ο **ό**νομ**ά** μου (My name) instead of just "όνομα".
